\section{Intro}\label{sec-intro}

This paper studies endogenous timing in a two-player Tullock contest
with asymmetric information. Labor and Management compete over policy
implementation, but only Labor privately observes how valuable victory
is to itself. Before effort is chosen, the order of moves matters:
moving first can serve as commitment, while moving second preserves
flexibility and allows a player to respond to its rival's action. The
central question is therefore simple but important: when one side has
private information about its prize value, which side wants to move
first, which side prefers to wait, and what kind of information is
revealed in equilibrium?

This question is relevant because many contest environments combine
strategic timing with informational asymmetry. In labor conflict,
electoral competition, lobbying, litigation, and rent-seeking more
generally, one side often knows more about its own stake in victory than
its rival does. That informational asymmetry affects effort incentives
directly, but it also affects the value of commitment and the value of
waiting. A player who moves early may reveal information through its
action; a player who waits may exploit the option value of responding to
observed behavior. Endogenous timing in contests is therefore not only a
question of strategic commitment, but also a question of how information
is transmitted through equilibrium play.

The natural benchmark is \citet{fu2006}, who studies endogenous timing
in a two-player contest with asymmetric information. The present paper
keeps that environment deliberately simple, but replaces the
one-dimensional informed type with a binary private-value structure. In
particular, Labor's prize value is either high or low around mean
\(\mu\), with dispersion governed by \(\delta\). This small modification
turns out to be analytically useful. It allows the model to separate the
role of the average private value from the role of information
dispersion, and therefore makes it possible to characterize timing
incentives at the type level rather than only at the aggregate level.

The paper delivers three main results. First, the informed-first subgame
admits a unique separating equilibrium. If Labor moves first and
Management responds after observing Labor's effort, then Labor's
equilibrium effort fully reveals whether its private value is high or
low. The reason is structural: Management's continuation best response
depends only on the observed effort and the known prize \(V\), not on
beliefs directly. Because beliefs cannot be used to punish deviations,
pooling cannot be sustained in the informed-first subgame.

Second, the binary structure yields a sharp characterization of timing
incentives. For each Labor type, the gain from waiting can be written as
an exact interval condition. This leads to a clean trichotomy: either
both types prefer to move first, only the low type prefers to wait, or
both types prefer to wait. The reverse pattern, in which only the high
type wants to wait, cannot arise. The model therefore not only
identifies when waiting is attractive, but also ranks which type has the
stronger second-mover incentive.

Third, mean and dispersion matter jointly. A comparison between the
average informed value \(\mu\) and Management's known value \(V\) is not
enough to determine timing. What matters is the interaction between
\(V\), the mean of Labor's private value, and the spread parameter
\(\delta\), which affects both beliefs and the distance between the two
informed types. In this sense, the binary extension is not merely a
discrete approximation to the original model; it changes what can be
said sharply about timing regions.

The contribution of the paper is therefore not greater generality in
player number or timing architecture. Its contribution is analytical
sharpness. Relative to \citet{fu2006}, the binary private-value
structure makes it possible to derive exact type-by-type timing
conditions, establish a strong no-pooling result in the informed-first
subgame, and show precisely how the mean and spread of private values
jointly determine equilibrium timing incentives.

The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. Section
Section~\ref{sec-fu} reviews the related literature and clarifies the
paper's contribution relative to \citet{fu2006} and subsequent work.
Section Section~\ref{sec-setup} introduces the environment and notation.
Section Section~\ref{sec-benchmarks} derives the simultaneous and
Management-first benchmarks. Section Section~\ref{sec-lm} analyzes the
informed-first subgame and establishes uniqueness of separation. Section
Section~\ref{sec-timing-incentives} characterizes type-by-type first-
versus second-mover incentives. Section
Section~\ref{sec-endogenous-timing} derives the implications for
endogenous timing. Section Section~\ref{sec-welfare} discusses welfare
comparisons, and Section Section~\ref{sec-summary} summarizes the main
equilibrium objects.

\section{Literature}\label{sec-fu}

\subsection{What the Current Model
Delivers}\label{what-the-current-model-delivers}

\citet{fu2006} is the closest benchmark. Relative to that paper, the
present binary-type specification yields a sharper characterization of
timing incentives under asymmetric information. In the LM subgame, the
informed Labor type chooses effort first and Management responds after
observing it. Because Management's best response depends only on the
observed effort and the known prize \(V\), off-path beliefs do not
affect continuation play. This eliminates the usual scope for
belief-based pooling support and implies that the LM subgame is uniquely
separating.

The binary structure also makes it possible to isolate two margins that
are intertwined in the one-type benchmark: the mean private value
\(\mu\) and the information range \(\delta\). Timing incentives are
therefore not governed by the comparison \(\mu\) versus \(V\) alone.
Instead, the key object is the interval condition in
Proposition~\ref{prp-exact-condition}, which implies that only three
timing profiles are feasible: both types move first, only the low type
waits, or both types wait.

\subsection{Relation to the Endogenous-Timing
Literature}\label{relation-to-the-endogenous-timing-literature}

The classic endogenous-timing literature implies that, in two-player
asymmetric contests, the weak player tends to move first and the strong
player follows. This logic originates in the broader endogenous-timing
framework of \citet{hamilton1990} and underlies the contest results in
\citet{fu2006}. More recent work qualifies that benchmark.
\citet{hoffmann2012} show that, with a general contest success function
and an endogenous prize, simultaneous play can re-emerge and leadership
need not be pinned down by the favorite-underdog comparison alone.
\citet{baikleelee2022} show that, in three-player Tullock contests,
moderate asymmetries can make simultaneous early play the unique
equilibrium, rather than a sequential pattern. In a more general
sequential all-pay setting, \citet{dengfuwuzhu2021} establish a robust
later-mover payoff advantage in richer multi-period timing
architectures.

Against that background, the present model contributes a tractable
two-player benchmark in which timing remains endogenous but private
information is concentrated on one side. The benefit of this restricted
environment is that it yields closed-form type-by-type conditions for
first- versus second-mover preferences, rather than only a qualitative
ranking of leadership incentives.

\subsection{Relation to the Asymmetric-Information and Signaling
Literature}\label{relation-to-the-asymmetric-information-and-signaling-literature}

The paper also sits in the broader literature on contests with private
information. \citet{hurley1998}, \citet{warneryd2003}, and
\citet{warneryd2012} study incomplete information in simultaneous
contest environments and show how private or common-value uncertainty
alters effort incentives. Within that literature, \citet{fu2006} is
again the closest reference because informed effort itself becomes a
signal. The present paper differs by showing that, under the binary
specification, signaling through effort does not merely \emph{allow}
separation; it makes separation the unique equilibrium outcome in the
informed-first subgame.

Other papers add an explicit communication or signaling stage before the
contest. \citet{fu2013} show that public messages can serve both as
commitment and as signals under asymmetric information, while
\citet{dentermorgansisak2022} show that costly pre-contest signals may
reveal relative talent only for sufficiently strong types. By contrast,
the current model does not rely on an extra communication technology.
The only signal is the informed player's equilibrium effort itself. This
keeps the mechanism close to \citet{fu2006} while delivering a stronger
no-pooling result.

\subsection{Relation to Newer
Extensions}\label{relation-to-newer-extensions}

Several newer papers show that the standard timing result can be
reversed once richer strategic frictions are introduced.
\citet{protopappas2023} studies how players may manipulate moves in
sequential contests through institutional changes that reshape first-
and second-mover incentives. \citet{protopappas2025} shows that, under
uncertainty about future effort costs, even the strong player may choose
to move first when information about those costs is asymmetric. In a
conflict setting, \citet{dentersisak2015} show that incomplete
information can weaken deterrence because a defender optimally
underinvests when it faces uncertainty about the opponent's resolve.

These papers are useful comparison points because they show that timing
predictions are sensitive to what is privately known: prize valuations,
future costs, strategic resolve, or institutional opportunities to
manipulate the order of moves. The contribution here is to isolate the
private-value channel and show that even a minimal binary information
structure is enough to generate rich timing regions and a strict
ordering of type-specific waiting incentives.

\subsection{Main Takeaway}\label{main-takeaway}

The literature review suggests that this paper is best read as a
theoretically sharp extension of \citet{fu2006}. Relative to the broader
contest literature, its main value is not greater generality in player
number or timing architecture, but sharper identification of when
private information creates first-mover or second-mover incentives. The
binary extension shows exactly how mean and dispersion jointly govern
timing, why only the low type can be the marginal waiter, and why the
informed-first subgame admits no pooling ambiguity.

\section{Setup}\label{sec-setup}

\subsection{Environment}\label{environment}

Two players, Labor (\(L\)) and Management (\(M\)), contest over whose
ideal policy is implemented. The outcome is winner-takes-all: the winner
receives its own prize value and the loser receives zero. The contest
technology follows \citet{tullock1980}, and the endogenous-timing
framework follows \citet{hamilton1990} and \citet{fu2006}.

\begin{definition}[Asymmetric Prize
Values]\protect\hypertarget{def-prizes}{}\label{def-prizes}

If Labor wins, Labor receives \(V_L > 0\) and Management receives \(0\).
If Management wins, Management receives \(V_M \equiv V > 0\) and Labor
receives \(0\). The two prize values need not coincide.

\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Binary Information
Structure]\protect\hypertarget{def-binary}{}\label{def-binary}

Labor privately observes its prize value before choosing timing or
effort. Management knows only that

\[
V_L \in \{V_\ell, V_H\}
  = \{\mu - \delta,\; \mu + \delta\},
\]

with probabilities \(1/2\) and \(1/2\), where \(\mu > \delta > 0\).
Hence \(\mu\) controls the mean of Labor's valuation and \(\delta\)
controls the range of private information.

\end{definition}

For later use, define

\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-u-types}{
u_\ell \equiv \sqrt{\mu-\delta}, \qquad
u_H \equiv \sqrt{\mu+\delta},
}\end{equation}

so that \(V_\ell = u_\ell^2\) and \(V_H = u_H^2\). The moments of
\(V_L\) under the binary distribution are

\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-moments-basic}{
\E[V_L] = \mu, \qquad
\Var(V_L) = \delta^2,
}\end{equation}

and the two expectation terms that enter Management's problem are

\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-moments}{
\kappa \equiv \E\!\left[\frac{1}{\sqrt{V_L}}\right]
  = \frac{1}{2u_\ell} + \frac{1}{2u_H},
\qquad
\nu \equiv \E\!\left[\sqrt{V_L}\right]
  = \frac{u_\ell + u_H}{2}.
}\end{equation}

\subsection{Contest Payoffs and Best
Responses}\label{contest-payoffs-and-best-responses}

With Tullock contest success function \(p_i = x_i/(x_L + x_M)\),
expected utilities net of effort costs are

\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-ul}{
U_L(x_L, x_M;\, V_L) = \frac{x_L}{x_L + x_M}\,V_L - x_L,
}\end{equation}

\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-um}{
U_M(x_L, x_M;\, V) = \frac{x_M}{x_L + x_M}\,V - x_M.
}\end{equation}

First-order conditions imply the best responses

\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-brl}{
x_L^*(x_M;\, V_L) = \sqrt{V_L x_M} - x_M,
}\end{equation}

\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-brm}{
x_M^*(x_L;\, V) = \sqrt{V x_L} - x_L.
}\end{equation}

Management's reaction Equation~\ref{eq-brm} depends only on the observed
effort \(x_L\) and the known prize \(V\). Beliefs about Labor's type do
not enter the second-stage best response directly. This observation
drives the separating-versus-pooling analysis below.

\section{Analysis}\label{sec-analysis}

\subsection{Benchmarks}\label{sec-benchmarks}

\subsubsection{Simultaneous Move}\label{simultaneous-move}

Both players choose effort simultaneously. Substituting
Equation~\ref{eq-brl} into Management's expected utility and taking
expectations over the binary distribution yields

\[
\E[U_M] = V\sqrt{x_M}\,\kappa - x_M,
\qquad
\frac{\partial \E[U_M]}{\partial x_M}
  = \frac{V\kappa}{2\sqrt{x_M}} - 1.
\]

Hence the simultaneous-move equilibrium is

\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-sim-efforts}{
x_M^{\mathrm{sim}} = \frac{V^2\kappa^2}{4}, \qquad
x_L^{\mathrm{sim}}(V_L)
  = \frac{V\kappa}{2}\sqrt{V_L} - \frac{V^2\kappa^2}{4}.
}\end{equation}

Equilibrium payoffs are

\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-ul-sim}{
U_L^{\mathrm{sim}}(V_L)
  = \left(\sqrt{V_L} - \frac{V\kappa}{2}\right)^2,
}\end{equation}

\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-um-sim}{
U_M^{\mathrm{sim}}(V_L)
  = \frac{V^2\kappa}{2\sqrt{V_L}} - \frac{V^2\kappa^2}{4}.
}\end{equation}

and expected payoffs become

\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-eul-sim}{
\E\!\left[U_L^{\mathrm{sim}}\right]
  = \mu - V\kappa\nu + \frac{V^2\kappa^2}{4},
}\end{equation}

\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-eum-sim}{
\E\!\left[U_M^{\mathrm{sim}}\right]
  = \frac{V^2\kappa^2}{4}.
}\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Management Moves First}\label{management-moves-first}

If Management commits before observing Labor's type, its problem is
identical to the simultaneous benchmark. Therefore

\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-ml-efforts}{
x_M^{ML} = \frac{V^2\kappa^2}{4}, \qquad
x_L^{ML}(V_L)
  = \frac{V\kappa}{2}\sqrt{V_L} - \frac{V^2\kappa^2}{4}.
}\end{equation}

Payoffs are again given by Equation~\ref{eq-ul-sim} through
Equation~\ref{eq-eum-sim}.

\begin{refremark}[No Additional Information Gain]
Subgame ML coincides with the simultaneous benchmark because Management
still cannot condition on Labor's private valuation. There is no extra
informational benefit from moving first.

\label{rem-ml}

\end{refremark}

\subsection{Labor First}\label{sec-lm}

Suppose Labor moves in period 1 and Management responds in period 2.
After observing \(x_L\), Management best-responds using
Equation~\ref{eq-brm}, so Labor type \(t \in \{\ell, H\}\) solves

\[
\max_{x_L \geq 0}\;
W_t(x_L)
\equiv
\frac{x_L}{x_L + x_M^*(x_L)}V_t - x_L
=
\frac{V_t}{\sqrt{V}}\sqrt{x_L} - x_L.
\]

The continuation payoff \(W_t\) is strictly concave in \(x_L\).

\subsubsection{Separating}\label{separating}

\begin{proposition}[Separating
Efforts]\protect\hypertarget{prp-separating}{}\label{prp-separating}

If type \(t\) chooses its own optimal first-stage effort, then

\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-lm-efforts}{
x_t^{S} = \frac{V_t^2}{4V},
\qquad
x_M^{S}(V_t) = \frac{V_t(2V - V_t)}{4V},
}\end{equation}

and payoffs are

\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-ul-lm}{
U_L^{S}(V_t) = \frac{V_t^2}{4V},
}\end{equation}

\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-um-lm}{
U_M^{S}(V_t) = \frac{(2V - V_t)^2}{4V}.
}\end{equation}

\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The first-order condition for type \(t\) is

\[
\frac{V_t}{2\sqrt{Vx_L}} - 1 = 0,
\]

which yields \(x_t^{S} = V_t^2/(4V)\). Substituting this effort into
Equation~\ref{eq-brm} gives the induced response by Management and the
two payoffs.
\end{proof}

\subsubsection{Pooling}\label{pooling}

\begin{proposition}[No Pooling
Equilibrium]\protect\hypertarget{prp-no-pool}{}\label{prp-no-pool}

There is no pooling perfect Bayesian equilibrium in the LM subgame.

\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Consider any candidate pooling effort \(x^P\) chosen by both types.
Because \(W_t(x_L)\) is strictly concave, each type has a unique optimal
effort, namely \(x_t^{S} = V_t^2/(4V)\). Since \(V_H \neq V_\ell\) when
\(\delta > 0\), these maximisers differ:

\[
x_H^{S} = \frac{(\mu+\delta)^2}{4V}
\neq
\frac{(\mu-\delta)^2}{4V} = x_\ell^{S}.
\]

Hence no common pooling effort \(x^P\) can be optimal for both types. At
least one type has a profitable deviation to its own maximiser.

Off-path beliefs cannot rescue pooling. Once a deviation \(x_L\) is
observed, Management's continuation choice is pinned down by
Equation~\ref{eq-brm}, which depends only on \(x_L\) and \(V\), not on
beliefs about which Labor type deviated. Therefore the deviating type
obtains the same payoff from deviating regardless of the off-path
belief.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Unique LM
Outcome]\protect\hypertarget{cor-unique-sep}{}\label{cor-unique-sep}

The unique perfect Bayesian equilibrium of the LM subgame is separating.
Labor's first-stage effort fully reveals whether \(V_L = V_\ell\) or
\(V_L = V_H\).

\end{corollary}

Expected payoffs in the unique LM equilibrium are

\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-eul-lm}{
\E\!\left[U_L^{S}\right]
  = \frac{\mu^2 + \delta^2}{4V},
}\end{equation}

\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-eum-lm}{
\E\!\left[U_M^{S}\right]
  = \frac{4V^2 - 4V\mu + \mu^2 + \delta^2}{4V}.
}\end{equation}

\subsection{Timing}\label{sec-timing-incentives}

\subsubsection{Incentives}\label{incentives}

For type \(t \in \{\ell, H\}\), define the gain from moving second
rather than first as

\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-rent-type}{
R_t \equiv U_L^{ML}(V_t) - U_L^{S}(V_t).
}\end{equation}

Thus \(R_t > 0\) means type \(t\) prefers to move second, while
\(R_t < 0\) means type \(t\) prefers to move first.

Writing \(u = \sqrt{V_t}\) and \(c \equiv V\kappa/2\), we can rewrite
the rent as

\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-rent-function}{
R(u)
=
\left(c-u-\frac{u^2}{2\sqrt{V}}\right)
\left(c-u+\frac{u^2}{2\sqrt{V}}\right).
}\end{equation}

\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-rent-positive}{
R(u) > 0
\iff
c < u - \frac{u^2}{2\sqrt{V}}
\quad \text{or} \quad
c > u + \frac{u^2}{2\sqrt{V}}.
}\end{equation}

Equivalently, type \(t\) prefers to move first if and only if

\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-first-move-interval}{
u_t - \frac{u_t^2}{2\sqrt{V}}
\leq
\frac{V\kappa}{2}
\leq
u_t + \frac{u_t^2}{2\sqrt{V}}.
}\end{equation}

\begin{proposition}[Exact Type-by-Type
Condition]\protect\hypertarget{prp-exact-condition}{}\label{prp-exact-condition}

Let \(c \equiv V\kappa/2\). Then type \(t\) prefers to move first if and
only if \(c\) lies in the interval

\[
I_t
\equiv
\left[
u_t - \frac{u_t^2}{2\sqrt{V}},
\;
u_t + \frac{u_t^2}{2\sqrt{V}}
\right].
\]

Equivalently, type \(t\) prefers to move second whenever \(c\) lies
strictly outside \(I_t\).

\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
This is just the factorisation in Equation~\ref{eq-rent-function}. The
rent is non-positive precisely when the two factors have opposite signs,
which is equivalent to Equation~\ref{eq-first-move-interval}.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Low Type Has the Stronger Incentive to
Wait]\protect\hypertarget{prp-low-more-wait}{}\label{prp-low-more-wait}

The low type always has at least as much second-mover rent as the high
type:

\[
R_\ell \geq R_H.
\]

with strict inequality whenever \(\delta > 0\).

\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let \(a = u_\ell\) and \(b = u_H\), so \(0 < a < b\) and
\(\kappa = \frac{1}{2a} + \frac{1}{2b}\). Direct algebra gives

\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-rent-difference}{
R_\ell - R_H
=
\frac{(b^2-a^2)\left[2(V-ab)^2 + ab(b-a)^2\right]}{4Vab}.
}\end{equation}

Every factor on the right-hand side is non-negative, and the first
factor is strictly positive when \(\delta > 0\). Hence \(R_\ell > R_H\)
for any non-degenerate binary distribution.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Only Three Timing Profiles Are
Possible]\protect\hypertarget{cor-three-profiles}{}\label{cor-three-profiles}

For Labor's two types, the only possible timing-preference profiles are:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\tightlist
\item
  both types prefer moving first: \(R_\ell \leq 0\);
\item
  only the low type prefers moving second: \(R_\ell > 0 \geq R_H\);
\item
  both types prefer moving second: \(R_H > 0\).
\end{enumerate}

The reverse pattern, in which only the high type prefers moving second,
is impossible.

\end{corollary}

\begin{refremark}[Mean and Range Jointly Matter]
The comparison between \(\mu\) and \(V\) is informative but not
sufficient by itself. The object that enters timing incentives is
\(c = V\kappa/2\), and \(\kappa\) depends on both the mean and the range
of the binary distribution. As a result, \(\mu < V\) does not by itself
rule out second-mover incentives, and \(\mu > V\) does not by itself
guarantee them. The exact criterion is the interval test in
Proposition~\ref{prp-exact-condition}.

\label{rem-mean-range}

\end{refremark}

\begin{refremark}[Role of the Range Parameter \(\delta\)]
For fixed \(\mu\), increasing \(\delta\) raises \(\kappa\) and hence
raises \(c = V\kappa/2\). At the same time it spreads the type-specific
intervals \(I_\ell\) and \(I_H\) farther apart. This is the sense in
which the range parameter controls whether the informed player wants to
move first or second. In the sandbox verification below, wider ranges
typically shift the economy from ``both wait'' to ``only the low type
waits'' and eventually to ``both move first''.

\label{rem-delta-range}

\end{refremark}

\subsubsection{Endogenous Timing}\label{sec-endogenous-timing}

\paragraph{Management's Incentive}\label{managements-incentive}

\begin{proposition}[Management's Timing
Preference]\protect\hypertarget{prp-mgmt}{}\label{prp-mgmt}

Management prefers moving first (Subgame ML) to moving second (Subgame
LM) if and only if

\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-mgmt-cond}{
V^3\kappa^2 \geq 4V^2 - 4V\mu + \mu^2 + \delta^2.
}\end{equation}

\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
This condition is exactly

\[
\E\!\left[U_M^{ML}\right] \geq \E\!\left[U_M^{S}\right],
\]

using Equation~\ref{eq-eum-sim} and Equation~\ref{eq-eum-lm}.
\end{proof}

\paragraph{Implications for Labor}\label{implications-for-labor}

The previous section implies a clean partition of Labor's timing
incentives.

\begin{corollary}[Timing Regions for the Informed
Player]\protect\hypertarget{cor-labor-regions}{}\label{cor-labor-regions}

For the informed player, the timing logic is:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\tightlist
\item
  both types prefer moving first when \(R_\ell \leq 0\);
\item
  only the low type prefers moving second when \(R_\ell > 0 \geq R_H\);
\item
  both types prefer moving second when \(R_H > 0\).
\end{enumerate}

\end{corollary}

\begin{refremark}[Mean and Range]
Relative to \citet{fu2006}, the binary-distribution extension isolates
two new mechanisms. First, the mean \(\mu\) shifts both type values and
the belief term \(\kappa\). Second, the spread \(\delta\) changes the
gap between \(u_\ell\) and \(u_H\) and therefore the overlap between
their first-mover intervals. Timing incentives are therefore jointly
governed by the mean and the range, not by the mean comparison alone.

\label{rem-fu-extension}

\end{refremark}

\begin{refremark}[No Pooling Ambiguity]
Because the LM subgame is uniquely separating, timing choices in the
endogenous game can be type-contingent without any pooling ambiguity.
When Equation~\ref{eq-mgmt-cond} also holds, Management is willing to
commit early; when it fails, Management prefers to wait for Labor's
revealed type.

\label{rem-no-pooling-ambiguity}

\end{refremark}

\subsection{Welfare}\label{sec-welfare}

Expected total welfare is

\[
\E[W] = \E[U_L] + \E[U_M].
\]

Therefore

\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-ew-ml}{
\E[W^{ML}] = \mu - V\kappa\nu + \frac{V^2\kappa^2}{2},
}\end{equation}

\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-ew-lm}{
\E[W^{S}] = V - \mu + \frac{\mu^2 + \delta^2}{2V}.
}\end{equation}

where the superscript \(S\) refers to the separating LM outcome.

The sign of \(\E[W^{ML}] - \E[W^{S}]\) depends jointly on \(V\),
\(\mu\), and \(\delta\). The binary extension therefore delivers a sharp
characterization of timing incentives without forcing a unique welfare
ranking across timing regimes.
